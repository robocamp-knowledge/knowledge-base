Testing the new LEGO Education Computer Science & AI set Part #1: 45-minute lessons  45-minute robotics lessons are not a new idea.  But they are becoming more and more important in real school timetables.  LEGO Education is now clearly positioning the new Computer Science & AI set around short, 45-minute classroom lessons — and honestly, we think this is the right direction.  ⏱️ Teachers need robotics activities that can fit into a regular school day.  But here is the key point:  A short lesson is not created by the box.  It is created by the lesson plan.  That is why we have been developing short SPIKE Prime lessons for some time already.  For example, in our SPIKE Prime Forklift lesson, students work with mechanics, movement logic and structured programming — all within a compact classroom format.  Now we are testing a similar idea with the new LEGO Education Computer Science & AI set.  Can you run a 45-minute lesson with the new set?  Yes.  But can you run several 45-minute lessons smoothly, one after another, during a normal school day?  That is a different question.  🔋 With SPIKE Prime, this part of the classroom workflow was simpler: one hub, one battery, one charging routine.  With the new Computer Science & AI electronics, each wireless component has its own battery. This opens up some interesting design possibilities, but it also means charging has to be planned before the lesson — it is not something teachers can always easily fix once the lesson has started.  So this is not really a question of whether the new set is “bad”.  It is not.  It is a question of what schools actually need.  If your goal is to run better 45-minute robotics lessons, you do not automatically need a new set.  You need a better lesson plan.  #RoboCamp #LEGOEducation #EducationalRobotics #STEMEducation #EdTech #CodingForKids #SPIKEPrime #ComputerScience #AIinEducation #HandsOnLearning

Content:
The recent announcement of LEGO Education’s Computer Science & AI sets raises an important question: should AI in robotics education focus on using AI tools, or on understanding how learning algorithms actually work?  Understanding how AI learns — through feedback, adaptation, and iteration — can already be taught today, even with robotics sets that many schools already have in their classrooms.  One example is Launcher AI+, an advanced SPIKE Prime activity developed by the RoboCamp team.  Students build a catapult-style robot that repeatedly launches a ball toward a target. After each attempt, the robot receives feedback — the shot was too short or too far — and adjusts its launch power accordingly. With every iteration, the program updates its parameters and gradually improves accuracy.  There is no black-box AI here. Students design the learning logic themselves and can clearly see how data and feedback shape the robot’s behavior over time.  This is what learning about AI can look like — without new hardware, and without using AI as a shortcut instead of understanding how it works.
